Textron (TXT) Q1 2018 Earnings Conference Call Transcript

TXT earnings call for the period ending March 31, 2018.

Motley Fool Staff
(the_motley_fool)
Apr 19, 2018 at 12:42PM

Image source: The Motley Fool.

Textron (NYSE:TXT)
Q1 2018 Earnings Conference Call
April 18, 2018 8:00 a.m. ET

Contents:

  • Prepared Remarks
  • Questions and Answers
  • Call Participants

Prepared Remarks:

Operator

Ladies and gentlemen, thank you for standing by, and welcome to the Textron First-Quarter 2018 Earnings Conference Call. [Operator instructions] As a reminder, today's conference is being recorded, and I would now like to turn the conference over to our host, Eric Salander, vice president of investor relations. Please go ahead.

Eric Salander -- Vice President, Investor Relations

Thanks, Brad, and good morning, everyone. Before we begin, I'd like to mention we will be discussing future estimates and expectations during our call today. These forward-looking statements are subject to various risks factors, which are detailed in our SEC filings and also in today's press release. On the call today, we have Scott Donnelly, Textron's chairman and CEO, and Frank Connor, our chief financial officer.

Our earnings call presentation can be found in the Investor Relations section of our website. Textron's s revenues in the quarter were $3.3 billion, up $203 million from last year's first quarter. Income from continuing operations was $0.72 per share, up from $0.37 per share, or $0.46 on an adjusted basis in last year's first quarter. Manufacturing cash flow before pension contributions reflected a use of cash of $158 million, compared to a use of cash of $227 million in last year's first quarter.

We also announced that we have reached a definitive agreement to sell our Tools & Test business to Emerson for approximately $810 million in cash. With that, I'll turn the call over to Scott.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Thanks, Eric, and good morning, everybody. Segment revenue was up in the quarter in industrial, Bell, and Aviation, partly offset by lower revenue in Systems, consistent with our expectations. The Bell revenues were up on higher military volumes in the quarter. On the commercial side, we delivered 46 helicopters, up from 27 in last year's first quarter.

We continue to see positive customer reaction to the 505 as we ramp production and deliveries, with the first units delivered to European customers in the quarter. We also continued to see international demand for the 412, closing two multi-aircraft orders. At Heli-Expo in February, we showcased our innovation leadership in the commercial helicopter market with the unveiling of the 407GXI, featuring a new Garmin avionic system and an upgraded FADEC-controlled Rolls-Royce engine. We also announced Air Methods Corporation as the launch customer with an order for six of these new aircraft.

On the new product front, following the first flight of the V-280 Valor in December, the aircraft continues to demonstrate its agility and maneuverability, hitting new milestones in the early stages of a flight-test program as it moves toward operation and full airplane mode. Each milestone represents another step forward in creating the next generation of tiltrotor aircraft. The aircraft was flown by a U.S. Army experimental test pilot, representing a great opportunity to put the aircraft in the hands of our military customer.

Moving to Systems. Revenues were down on lower volumes, primarily at weapons and sensors related to the discontinuance of the SFW production line. During the quarter, unmanned systems received two task orders for the Aerosonde with a combined value of $97 million from NAVAIR in addition to a $28 million logistics support contract for the U.S. Army.

At marine and land systems, we remain in good progress on the Ship to Shore program as the first craft began on-water testing over this month and will proceed to build those trials next with deliveries scheduled for the summer. At TRU Simulation + Training, we signed a purchase agreement for an A350 full-flight sim with Airbus. And we're also chosen to provide the first-ever full-flight simulators for HR 600 aircraft models for Japan Air Commuter and Ansett Aviation Training of Australia. At industrial, we saw 14% increase in revenues, with growth in each of our businesses.

Overall margin was down, reflecting the additional Arctic Cat operations in the quarter as compared to the prior year due to the March 2017 timing of the acquisition and the first-quarter seasonality of the outdoor power sports business. We saw higher volumes in our growing GSE product line and also received the first order for the innovative new TUG Alpha 4 aircraft pushback, working five units for delivery to UPS later this year. Within our Textron off-road product line, we unveiled the new Wildcat XX side by side designed with an off-road racing-inspired suspension system, delivering best-in-class performance. At Arctic Cat, we introduced our new Mountain snowmobiles with our groundbreaking Alpha One single-beam rear-suspension system, further demonstrating our industry-leading product innovation.

Moving to Textron Aviation. Revenues were up 4% in the quarter. We delivered 36 jets, up from 35 last year, and 29 commercial turboprops, up from 20 last year. These deliveries reflect an improved mix of models across our jet and commercial turboprop product lines.

We saw continued order strength in the quarter with a number of multi-aircraft orders, including five Caravans for service into Papua New Guinea and two special mission King Airs configured for an air ambulance fleet in Australia. In February, we attended the Singapore Airshow where we announced Latitude has been granted certifications in China and Australia, demonstrating our commitment to the Asia Pacific region. We also had our first two Latitude deliveries into China in the quarter. For the second consecutive year, the Latitude earned the title as for the most delivered business jet in the midsize category.

On the new-product front, we continued to work toward certification of the Longitude, which we expect later in the second quarter. Our Longitude made its APAC debut at the Singapore Airshow, successfully circumnavigating the globe and demonstrating its commanding performance and reliability to customers along the way. We also made progress in the Sky Courier, successfully completing wind-tunnel testing as we look to develop this aircraft on an accelerated time line. The Denali is tracking its development schedule and continues to generate a significant amount of customer interest.

Our AT-6 Wolverine aircraft was chosen by the U.S. Air Force to participate in the second phase of the light attack experiment program, which begins in the second quarter. Lastly, we have agreed to sell our Tools & Test business to Emerson. While Tools & Test has been an important part of our Industrial portfolio for many years, this transaction represents a good return for our shareholders and positions the business for future success as part of Emerson.

With that, I'll turn the call over to Frank.

Frank T. Connor -- Executive Vice President and Chief Financial Officer

Thank you, Scott, and good morning, everyone. Segment profit in the quarter was $279 million, up $60 million from the first quarter of 2017 on a $203 million increase in revenues. Let's review how each of the segments contributed, starting with Textron Aviation. At Textron Aviation, revenues were up $40 million from this period last year, primarily due to higher price and volume.

Segment profit was $72 million, up from $36 million a year ago due to favorable volume and mix, performance, and price. Backlog in the segment ended the quarter at $1.6 billion. Moving to Bell. Revenues were up $55 million, primarily due to higher military volume, partially offset by lower commercial revenues due to the mix of aircraft sold.

Segment profit increased $4 million from the first quarter in 2017, primarily reflecting the higher volume. Backlog in the segment was $3.6 billion at the end of the quarter. At Textron Systems, revenues were down $29 million, primarily due to lower volume at weapons and sensors related to the discontinuance of SFW production in 2017. Segment profit was up $30 million, primarily reflecting improved performance at marine and land.

Backlog in the segment was $1.4 billion. Industrial revenues increased $139 million, largely related to favorable foreign exchange, the Arctic Cat acquisition, and higher volumes. Segment profit was down $12 million despite the increase in revenues from the first quarter of 2017 due to the timing of the Arctic Cat acquisition in the prior year. Finance segment revenues decreased $2 million and segment profit increased $2 million compared to last year's first quarter.

Moving below segment profit. Corporate expenses were $27 million and interest expense was $34 million, both flat with last year. We also repurchased 5.9 million shares, returning $344 million in cash to shareholders. To wrap up with guidance.

We are reiterating our expected full-year EPS from continuing operations of $2.95 to $3.15 per share. We also continue to expect cash flow from continuing operations of the manufacturing group before pension contributions of $700 million to $800 million. This guidance includes the expected impact of the Tools & Test divestiture on earnings per share and cash flow from continuing operations. That concludes our prepared remarks, so Brad, we can open the line for questions.

Questions and Answers:

Operator

Of course. [Operator instructions] We do have a question from the line of Carter Copeland with Melius Research. Please go ahead

Carter Copeland -- Melius Research -- President

Good morning, gentlemen.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Good morning.

Any color you can give us there will be helpful.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, it's kind of all of the above, Carter, and I think our volume obviously was up a little bit, but the mix was favorable there. Teams continue to do a good job on the performance side in terms of cost and therefore delivering better margin on some of the models. And as we've been talking about, we continue to push for incremental gains on the price side and we saw that as well. It certainly helped to have some more turboprop.

As you know, a year ago the turboprop was particularly light and so we were pleased to see some volume coming back in both the King Air and Caravan lines as well.

Carter Copeland -- Melius Research -- President

And on the after-market?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

So after-market was good performance. You'll see in the numbers just from a percent standpoint, not as large increases we've been seeing and that's largely because we've had a small change driven by some of 606 in terms of how we treat some engine programs. OK. And then one last one and I'll give -- the order momentum through the quarter, I mean, I know January is always a light month and February can be as well. Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, we think it remains pretty strong. And you're right, usually the first quarter, January, February pretty quiet. We continue to see relatively strong order bookings in that. So it's driven positive book-to-bill in -- across both jet and turboprop lines, which is good.

Carter Copeland -- Melius Research -- President

OK. Thanks, gentlemen.

Sure.

Operator

And we do have a question from the line of Sam Pearlstein with Wells Fargo. Please go ahead.

Sam Pearlstein -- Wells Fargo Securities -- Analyst

Goodmorning.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Good morning, Sam.

Sam Pearlstein -- Wells Fargo Securities -- Analyst

Scott, you've talked a lot, I guess, over the years about just driving earnings growth and seemed reluctant, it seems, to divest some of the lower-return businesses because of earnings dilution. And I guess, just based on the early reaction, certainly stockholders seem to like this, but -- given the buyback activity. But does this influence your views in terms of thinking about the mix of businesses, the portfolio, whether it makes sense to get out of certain lower-return businesses?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, I'm not sure -- I wouldn't categorize the Tools & Test business as a low-return business. I think that our stated position with respect to any disposition is that we're going to run these businesses to the best of our ability and make the right investments in them, and we certainly have done that in the Tools & Test segment, both organically as well as acquisition and with the intent to make it a great business. On the other hand, if somebody comes along and says, "Look, we value that business and think it's a better fit in our portfolio," and put something on the table that we think is good for our shareholders as well as something obviously they think is good for their shareholders, then we're open to entertaining a transaction, and that's exactly what happened here. Tools & Test is a good business.

I think it's well-positioned in the marketplace. It's performed well for us. But I think when you look at the, Emerson and who serves much larger footprint obviously into that kind of consumer base and channel, they feel that it's -- would be a better part of their business. And so I think that allowed a transaction to happen that is good for Textron and Textron shareholder and in all likelihood a very good thing for Emerson and Emerson shareholders as well.

Sam Pearlstein -- Wells Fargo Securities -- Analyst

OK. And I think when you started the year, you usually put a placeholder in for making acquisitions yourself and that if you don't find any, we see a step-up in the buyback. So if I just think about the first quarter's buyback plus this, can you comment at all about the acquisition pipeline or what you're seeing as it would seem like you probably aren't seeing a lot that's going to come to fruition?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, Sam, we always keep an eye out for transactions. I mean, I don't think there's any particularly large thing out there that I see on the horizon. There's always a number of things that we're looking at that are logical bolt-ons to the businesses that we have today. But in general, though, Sam, I would say, I think that given our balance sheet position, we are very comfortable to proceed with the level of buyback activity that we've talked about.

Obviously, that will step up in terms of the use of the cash from the disposition. If something comes along that makes sense for us, I think we have plenty of room to be able to manage that on our balance sheet through debt financing and stuff like that. So I think we're very comfortable with the level of buyback that we have in our plan regardless of whether M&A opportunities pop up or not.

Sam Pearlstein -- Wells Fargo Securities -- Analyst

OK. Thank you.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Sure.

Operator

And we do have a question from the line of Sheila Kahyaoglu with Jefferies.

Sheila Kahyaoglu -- Jefferies & Company -- Senior Vice President

Maybe if you could further elaborate on the Aviation backlog, sort of what drove the strength there. It was up $400 million on a sequential basis. Was that mostly Longitude jumping in? And then if you could just comment on the certification and is it expected later in Q2 and delivery starting in Q3?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

So as you know, we don't do a model-by-model backlog. I would say if you look at the Textron Aviation backlog, Sheila, there was some increase on the defense side, really largely driven by 606. There were some service contracts that were longer term, which are now all booked in there. But we had strong book-to-bill on the jet product line as a whole and we had a strong book-to-bill on the turboprop line as a whole.

So it was really, I would say, pretty strong bookings across all segments of the business. In terms of Longitude, we do expect it to certify here in the quarter. We're kind of in last flights. There were a couple of delays that we would like not to have had, but it's nothing serious, everything is fine.

Well, we just had some test flights to accomplish and some last builds, so everything is going well and we would expect that will happen here pretty soon. Whether we get one into the second quarter or the first sales happen in the third quarter is still sort of TBD. I mean, the nature of this aircraft, obviously, is it does have a little more customization on the interiors than a lot of other aircraft, so it really will just depend on what timeline we get the final of a -- if we're done and whether we can turn that around in time to get it in Q2, or certainly with worst case, fall into Q3.

Sheila Kahyaoglu -- Jefferies & Company -- Senior Vice President

Got it. And then just one on your military portfolio. Maybe if you could comment on my favorite two programs, the Scorpion and Future Vertical Lift, sort of what the timelines and hurdles look like for those two?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, on the Scorpion, we're continuing some international customer activity, we just had a customer in a couple of weeks ago, so those dialogues continue. On Future Vertical Lift, we continue a lot of dialogue, obviously, with the Army and the Marine Corps. We've had the Army now flying the aircraft. They're certainly very bought into this program and a very active partner in its development.

We're working, really, at this stage of the game on envelope expansion, which is going very nicely, so the team are doing a great job on that, and the Army's following it very carefully. Now, how this all works out through their budget cycles and, still remains to be determined. I think obviously our view is that we could deliver aircraft and a lot of capability at a much faster rate than what's currently budgeted into the Army budget and just sort of the general thinking in terms of how long it takes to develop a new aircraft like this and, but we're just going to continue to do our thing and demonstrate the maturity and capability of the aircraft and go from there.

Sheila Kahyaoglu -- Jefferies & Company -- Senior Vice President

Sounds good. Thank you.

Operator

And we do have a question from the line of Peter Arment with Baird. Please go ahead.

Peter J. Arment -- Robert W. Baird & Company -- Analyst

Good morning, Scott and Frank.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Good morning.

Peter J. Arment -- Robert W. Baird & Company -- Analyst

On Textron Systems, really nice margin performance there. How should we think about that? I mean, you mentioned land and marine is improving, but it seemed pretty significantly well above our expectations. Are you -- is this kind of outlook sustainable? Or how should we think about the overall performance there?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, I don't think this margin rate is sustainable through the course of the year. I think it will revert, and we feel pretty good about our guidance in terms of what we gave you guys. So we'll see some lower margin rates as we move on through the course of the year. But on the other hand, I think the team is performing well.

TAPV, we've talked about a lot over the years and we're just sort of working our way through those last deliveries. The program is executing as we expected. On Ship to Shore, the team is doing well. I mean, obviously, we're in integration test, which is always a challenging time, but the team is making great progress.

As I said, we actually have had the aircraft on the water. So it's operating and is going to enter into builder's trials here pretty soon. And of course, the good news is on the appropriations, authorized -- or appropriate levels of the craft are in great shape and we're working with our customer right now on that production proposal and we would expect that we would have something signed later this year and be able to start to see production moving. We already have some long-lead material and advanced activity going on to make sure that there's no line break as part of this process.

And I say we have worked well with the customer to make sure that's the case, but we're working through the details now on the first production contract.

Peter J. Arment -- Robert W. Baird & Company -- Analyst

That's helpful, and just a quick follow-up. On Bell, the deliveries were up really nicely year over year -- 505, 407 continue to be strong. What are you seeing in that particular end market that's really driving this?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, I mean, we've really seen strength around the world, particularly the light ends, the 505, as you see, well, that's really just been us having to ramp production and meet the demand for that product, which has been very strong. So we're obviously very pleased to see the production rates coming up and the number of units delivered going up. 407 continues to be strong. I think the GXI also was well-received and we have strong demand in there as well.

412s and 429s, as you know, particularly 412s, tend to be more back-end-loaded just in terms of deliveries and I think that's certainly what we expect we'll see over the course of the year.

Peter J. Arment -- Robert W. Baird & Company -- Analyst

Thanks, Scott.

Operator

And we do have a question from the line of Noah Poponak with Goldman Sachs. Please go ahead.

Good morning, Noah.

Noah Poponak -- Goldman Sachs -- Analyst

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Well, look, Noah, we're certainly not going to go into any more granular breakdown in the backlog, but I'd say that we're -- we had a strong book-to-bill in Citation jets, including all of our legacy models as well, as well as in the turboprop side, which is inclusive of both the King Air and the Caravan. So I think market demand continues to be strong and we see that reflected in our book-to-bill and backlog.

Noah Poponak -- Goldman Sachs -- Analyst

So is it safe to assume the in-production jet bookings compared to revenue was in the zone of what the total segment was?

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

Yes.

Noah Poponak -- Goldman Sachs -- Analyst

Got it. And then on the pricing discussion there, can you help us out with sort of order of magnitude there? I mean, what kind of pricing increases are you seeing at this point? And I guess, how much has price come up off the trough at this point? And where I'm going is sort of if backlog starts to build, it'll be interesting to see how you decide to -- you get asked the question if you'll raise production, but it seems like you would maybe actually just potentially let price keep going for some period of time, given the success you're having there and what that does to earnings in the segment before you raise production.

Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

I think that's true, Noah. Look, we have been saying that we will trade price for volume. I think these aircraft, I mean, I can't tell you we're going back to trough, I guess. I mean, we look at the data on more of a year-over-year or incremental subsequent-quarter basis, which is how we measure our sales teams obviously.

So as we look at the price targets and the performance of that, we are putting that higher on the list than just driving volume in this aircraft. And we are getting price increases, we continue to get price increases, but they're still at price levels that we're not very happy about. I mean, look, we, as a team, did a great job in the quarter. I think we're continuing to execute well on a lot of fronts.

But the amount of capital, the amount of investments that you make in this business warrants getting a better return and we need to see better pricing in the end market. And so that's the trade we're making. We are walking, we have been walking away from deals that are at price levels that are just not acceptable to business.

Noah Poponak -- Goldman Sachs -- Analyst

Got it, really helpful. Just one other quick one. The Bell -- if I take the Bell commercial and military units and price on the OE side plus the comment that total commercial revenue was down, which kind of tells me roughly where commercial after-market was, it seems like military after-market was up like 75% or something like that. Scott C. Donnelly -- Chairman, President, and Chief Executive Officer

No. I mean, I can't follow exactly your math, but the military after-market was up, but it was certainly not up anywhere near the percent you're talking. That -- again, over the course of the year, it's not going to make a whole heck of a lot of difference, but the way it falls out in the year, there's a little bit stronger revenue on the military side here in Q1 than you'll see through the balance of year because it's a little flatter given the way the accounting is done under 606 as opposed to the delivery that we've historically had.

Noah Poponak -- Goldman Sachs -- Analyst

Got it. OK. Thanks so much.

Sure.
