Mihael Polymeropoulos -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Thank you, Jim. Good afternoon. 2018 has been a year of great accomplishments and growth for Vanda in both commercial and research and development. Before I review our achievements and share our plans for 2019, I would like to address the importance of last week's announcement and how it makes our Company stronger for our patients and shareholders.

Last week, we announced that Vanda filed a complaint against the Food and Drug Administration asking the court to set aside a partial clinical hold affecting certain proposed studies of 20%. The FDA imposed this partial clinical hold on two proposed studies. The first was a proposed 52-week open label extension period for patients who had completed the tradipitant Phase II clinical study, 2301, in gastroparesis. The second was a new follow-on 52-week open label extension protocol, 2302, for patients who had completed the 2301 study.

In imposing the partial clinical hold, the FDA stated that we had required to conduct an additional chronic toxicity study in dogs, monkeys or mini pigs before allowing patients access in any clinical protocol beyond 12 weeks. The partial clinical hold was not based on any safety or efficacy data related to tradipitant, rather the FDA informed Vanda that this additional toxicity study is required by guidance document. We do not expect the partial clinical hold to have any material impact on our ongoing clinical studies in atopic dermatitis and motion sickness or the planned Phase III study in gastroparesis.

Further, the partial clinical hold has not had any impact on the potential timing of an NDA filing or approval for these indications. We will continually reassess the situation as events unfold. Please note, drug safety is our top priority and we fully support conducting tests that are both effective and useful. As we have said, there has been great progress in technology for research in this area since the FDA first imposed this requirement. In fact technology exists today that we may be able to leverage and get real safety information that we could use instead of this chronic dog studies, especially given their poor or non-existent predictive value toward human safety.

For example, the FDA is already partnering with industry to evaluate and qualify organ-on-chip technology for toxicology testing to meet regulatory standards for products that include foods, dietary supplements and cosmetics.

While there is a place for animal studies in drug development, international consensus, including the FDA has upheld the principle that these studies should only be conducted when absolutely necessary, so that we continue to improve on the free hours reduce, refine and replace animal experimentation. As you may know, tradipitant has already been extensively studied in preclinical and clinical programs, and in all studies, it has been well tolerated by animals and humans with no significant safety findings.

In preclinical models, tradipitant has been studied in mice, rats and dogs in short-term and long-term studies. These studies include a three-month duration study of rats, a six-month duration study in rats again, and a three-month duration study in dogs. None of these studies resulted in any significant safety findings observed at low, medium or high dose exposures. In the three-month dog study, no significant safety findings were identified at any of the doses studied including in the high dose of 1500 milligrams per kilogram per day, which is more than 300 times the proposed human equivalent dose. For the three-month dog study, the absence of any significant effects included clinical, laboratory, microscopic and macroscopic observations.

Additionally, tradipitant belongs to a class of compounds known as neurokinin-1 receptor antagonist with the most well-known member being aprepitant. As seen with tradipitant, aprepitant was also shown to be well tolerated across a number of studies, acute and chronic, across different doses and across a number of species include in mice, rats, dogs and primates. Tradipitant has also been studied in number of clinical studies across diverse patient populations for durations up to three months, again, with no significant safety findings. Moreover, tradipitant was recently shown to be efficacious in a study with patients with gastroparesis, making it one of the most promising candidates for a new treatment for gastroparesis in the last 40 years.

We're also seeking to develop tradipitant for the treatment of atopic dermatitis and motion sickness. As such, tradipitant could be an important new treatment option for millions of patients. This significant body of evidence that consistently demonstrates the benign safety profile of tradipitant prompted Vanda to seek agreement with the FDA that a nine-month duration study in non-rodents recommended by guidance is not necessary to conduct. Moreover, Vanda sights (ph) with the overwhelming scientific evidence that such a chronic study in dogs is unnecessary given its poor or non-existent predictive value for human safety. The purpose of preclinical safety animal studies is to predict safety issues that may be seen in human studies before they happen.

Most of the chronic studies identify signals previously seen in shorter duration studies, and when rarely they identify new signal, it is often seen in either clinical or laboratory evaluations as well and almost never results (inaudible) in the direction of the clinical program. This lack of utility of chronic dog studies has been acknowledged by international regulatory bodies in the course of development of agricultural chemicals, leading to removal of a chronic dog study requirement from the list of required studies by the Environmental Protection Agency in the US.

While Vanda's primary goal is to use innovation in the service of people's pursuit of happiness, we're also mindful of the means by which we accomplish these goals, including animal welfare and patient autonomy and safety. We are confident that an experienced solution will be identified as solution that will make our program stronger and that establishes Vanda as a leader in the socially responsible development of pharmaceuticals.

Let me now turn to the review of 2018 and address our great accomplishments in both the commercial activities as well as our research and development efforts, as we look forward a successful 2019. Our efforts to commercialize tasimelteon for Non-24 were expanded in 2018 with the aim to create broader awareness of the disorder among psychiatrists. As it is known, Non-24 is commonly present among totally blind individuals, but it is also found with low prevalence among sighted individuals. This low prevalence of Non-24 among sighted individuals, presents a significant challenge in identifying physicians who will likely see this patients. However, Non-24 is reported to be co-morbid with certain conditions that are typically treated by psychiatrists. These include bipolar disorder, depressive disorders and traumatic brain injury.

In the fourth quarter of 2017, we launched a new initiative, HPI where our Fanapt psychiatric sales force began creating awareness for Non-24 and HETLIOZ among psychiatrists. HPI has been a productive initiative through 2018, significantly increase in demand, when monthly new scripts increasing by approximately three-fold as compared to months prior to the HPI launch.

At the same time, our targeted efforts to facilitate treatment of blind patients with Non-24 continues. This very significant increase in demand has led to continued growth of the number of patients from HETLIOZ, despite the significant hurdles that patients face with third-party payers. Vanda has strengthened the supportive patients and doctors in navigating this payer hurdles through the newly established initiative of Vanda benefits. Most payers require prior authorization before approving HETLIOZ for Non-24 and will -- while some quickly authorize payment after that, many do so until many length cycles of appeals. This lengthy and arduous process harms patients and Vanda will continue to enhance our efforts to help patients access this much needed treatment. 2019 marks our five-year anniversary since the FDA approval of HETLIOZ in 2014, and we're pleased that more and more patients with Non-24 continue to.

On Fanapt, our effort is focused on creating awareness among psychiatrists that treat patients with schizophrenia. Our sales force has been successful in stabilizing the level of scripts, which in past years experienced a small but steady decline. We are confident that as psychiatrists are becoming more familiar with Fanapt, they will expand its use in patients with schizophrenia who suites from other antipsychotic. We will continue to strengthen our marketing efforts of Fanapt in 2019 addressing both patient and physician audiences.

I will now turn to our research and development efforts and some key highlights for 2019. On Fanapt, iloperidone, we have made significant progress in the development of a one-month long-acting injectable formulation of iloperidone with a pharmacokinetic study under way. In this PK study, we're evaluating the pharmacokinetic properties of different doses of LAI formulation over a one-month period. We plan to complete the PK study in the coming months and initiate an efficacy study of LAI formulation in patients with schizophrenia later this year. We plan to also initiate an efficacy study of oral iloperidone in patients with bipolar disorder in the coming months.

On HETLIOZ, tasimelteon,we reported positive results in a number of studies of our jet lag program last year, including two efficacy studies and the results of the driving study. We successfully filed a supplemental NDA for the indication with a PDUFA date of August 16, 2019.

In anticipation of approval of HETLIOZ jet lag, we're well under way with our launch preparations to ensure broad access to the millions of travelers every year that experience jet lag. Our program will aim to address all populations that experience jet lag including frequent business travelers, as well as leisure travelers. Our commercialization approach of HETLIOZ will utilize consumer product methods in order to properly acknowledge the uniqueness of the setting of this condition.

Additionally, we also reported positive results for this Smith-Magenis clinical study getting us closer to a supplemental NDA filing. We are preparing to meet with the FDA in the near future and we hope to file a supplemental NDA in the coming months. Smith-Magenis Syndrome or SMS is a rare orphan genetic disorder that affects an estimated 1 in 50,000 to 1 in 25,000 individuals in the US. Tasimelteon aims to address the most common symptom of the disorder, which is extreme sleep disturbance, which is associated with significant disruption in the lives of these patients and their families.

We have also initiated an observational study in patients with delayed sleep phase disorder who carry a mutation in the CRY1 ones gene. A mutation in the CRY1 gene has been recently described as responsible for cases of delayed sleep phase disorder or DSPD and it is estimated that given the population frequency of up to 0.8% of this CRY1 exon 11 deletion, this may be a significant contributor to the DSPD phenotype. A Phase II study in the DSPD CRY1 exon 11 deletion carriers is expected to begin later this year.

Tradipitant, in 2018, we initiated a Phase III study of tradipitant in atopic dermatitis and we reported positive results of tradipitant in a Phase II study of patients with gastroparesis. For atopic dermatitis, we're currently recruiting patients in a 500-patient Phase III study to evaluate the efficacy and safety of tradipitant in an eight-week randomized period during which patients will receive tradipitant 85 milligrams twice a day or placebo in a randomized fashion.

We're evaluating a number of measures of improvement, including patient reported measures of worst itch who daily dairies, as well as objective measures that include (inaudible). At this time, we expect that this study will be completed and top line results will be reported by mid-2020.

We also have plans to initiate the second Phase III study in atopic dermatitis likely by the first quarter of 2020. For gastroparesis, we reported positive Phase II study results with success on a number of endpoints, particularly on measures of patient reported daily nausea and the measure of nausea free days. We're encouraged by these results and plan to initiate a Phase III study in the second quarter of this year in order to confirm these findings.

We will be soon planning to meet with the FDA and discuss our proposed clinical program at an end of Phase II meeting.

We have recently initiated a third indication program for tradipitant for the treatment of patients with motion sickness. This current protocol involves the induction of motion sickness by sea travel to patients with history of motion sickness. We expect completion of this Phase II study and report of results by end of the second quarter of this year. We believe that the success of tradipitant in this programs has the potential for the development of significant therapeutic solutions for patients with a series of indications.

For atopic dermatitis, tradipitant could become a systemic antipruritic agent used across a spectrum of disease severity with accompanying improvement on skin lesions. For gastroparesis, tradipitant could become the first new agent for gastroparesis over 40 years, and for motion sickness, if successful, tradipitant has the potential to address a significant unmet need for millions of people and billions of trips per year.
