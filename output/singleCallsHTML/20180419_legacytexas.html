During the first quarter, non-performing loans dropped $44.6 million, which was driven by a $43 million decline in non-performing energy loans. Our capital is quite strong. TCE has grown to 9.22% and our Tier 1 common risk-based capital was at 9.91%.Turning to Slide 5, I would note that gross loans held for investments were up 1.3% on a linked-quarter basis, while both total deposits and non-interest bearing deposits were up 2.8% linked quarter. Finally, as we mentioned a couple of quarters ago, we plan to reduce the overall commitments to shared national credits, particularly large mix, and focus on our own originations and more clubby [ph] deals.

These changes to the deposit mix have increased the bank's asset sensitivity. Our cost of deposits, including non-interest-bearing demand deposits, increased to 73 basis points in 2018, up from 64 basis points in 2017.Slide 10 shows net interest income for the first quarter of $78.6 million, a decrease of $1.6 million linked quarter and $2.1 million higher than first quarter 2017. Q1 2017 efficiency ratio was favorably impacted by the previously mentioned energy loan discount recognized in interest income.Turning to Slide 12, you will see our non-performing loans decreased by $44.6 million, or 47%, for the quarter to $49.8 million, while our total classified assets, including foreclosed assets, decreased $17.9 million for the same period. We had net charge-offs of $12.4 million and booked a $15.7 million provision for credit losses for Q1 '18. Our allowance for loan losses grew to $74.5 million at March 31, 2018, compared to $71.3 million at December 31, 2017. We ended the quarter with the allowance for loan loss equal to 1.2% of total loans held for investment, excluding acquired and warehouse-purchase program loans.

As of March 31, 2018, the allowance allocated to our $547.3 million energy loan portfolio was $17 million, while the allowance allocated to our $38.8 million corporate healthcare finance portfolio was $10.9 million.Slide 13 shows the strengthening of our capital position on March 31, 2018. I will highlight here that our Basel III Tier 1 common ratio is estimated at 9.9%. We ended the quarter with 9.2% TCE to total assets and a 9.6% Tier 1 leverage ratio. Yeah. And I'll save my energy comments until somebody asks about that, but the healthcare bucket, Brady, today stands at $38 million. Kevin Hanigan -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Yeah. Really fair question, Brady. So when I look at the remaining problem loans in the bank, and for anybody who's got the earnings release in front of them, on Page 7, we've got kind of a recap of what is going on in the criticized and classified loan portfolio of the bank. Our NPAs did drop from $94 million down to $49 million in the quarter, and that was two energy credits that got resolved, $37 million energy credit and then another one that's about 7.

The one that was $7 million resolved within the impairment mark that we had on it, almost exactly. There was like an $18,000 difference between the impairments and the settlement amount on that one.On the larger one, we had an impairment on that one of a little over $5 million and it ended up being a $10.5 million loss on that $37 million credit. The assets were sold to a local group out in Fort Worth, well-heeled, and in the process of the sale, they have agreed to put up significant dollars to fix up some deferred maintenance [Inaudible] in the fields that they're operating in and then this company had very significant reserves in another area that they were interested in and they committed to putting significant money in for drilling out that field and improving the value. But because the trade occurred, and we financed the trade, that asset moved, Brady, from a non-performing loan up into the next category, which is a classified performing loan.

That $27 million was the $37 million less the $10 million loss that moved into that. We believe, and they've already started drilling and fixing up the field, they'll add significant values to this and that one should migrate into a better position.So what's left to energy are two credits, a $14 million credit that's up for sale and then the smaller one, which is $1.4 million. So as I said, the next biggest loan asset in the NPA bucket is that $12.3 million healthcare deal.

So if you think about that, that's 26 of the remaining $49 million in deals, one of them is very, very reserved for. And the sales process is really on the front end of that. I don't know that it gets resolved in this quarter, and maybe the third quarter but as I just look at the remaining loss potential in the bank, I think it lies in that $14 million deal. And I'm not saying there is a loss position because we don't have a mark on it based upon the asset values we've got in this initial bid we got, but it hasn't played out. That one has yet to play out.

Brady Gailey -- Keefe, Bruyette & Woods -- Managing Director

OK. And then lastly just for Mays, I know you've talked about the expense run rate kind of in the $41 million to $42 million range, came a little higher than that this quarter, as you mentioned. How should we think about quarterly expenses going forward?

Mays Davenport -- Chief Financial Officer

Yes. For the quarter, we had $43.9 million. As I mentioned in the prepared comments, the $1.1 million linked-quarter increase in payroll taxes and the 679,000 one-time bonus shouldn't repeat. So if you take that out, you would get to, what I would consider, a more normal run rate for the rest of the year.

Operator

And the next question comes from Michael Rose with Raymond James.

Michael Rose -- Raymond James -- Managing Director

Just a quick question. Can you walk through the reclassification of the warehouse? I didn't really understand, just how does that work?

Kevin Hanigan -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Yeah. So in the warehouse business, we had about $145 million worth of credit that was outstanding at the end of Q1 and that was closer to 160 at year-end that are lines of credit. So these are warehouse-related deals, Michael, that we don't take a 100% participation in the credit. These are bigger credits that we've banked for a long period of time that have moved into borrowing money without doing the 100% participation.

They pledge the assets under a typical bank revolver. As such, we've historically kept that roughly $150 million worth of that business in the C&I bucket and because of the volatility it creates within C&I -- it can move a lot between any given quarter -- I think it can give a false read about the growth or performance of our C&I side of our business. So we elected to move that out of the C&I bucket and into the warehouse bucket. They really are in the same exact business.

It's just they're financed slightly differently in that we have a security interest in the asset rather than the ownership of the asset. And technically all that really does when you have the ownership, it makes that asset bankruptcy-proof, if you will. So you won't, you don't have to fight for it in a bankruptcy. Again, these credits are very large now.

Our guy who runs that business has banked them for 25 or 30 years and we have stuck with them as they've grown out of the traditional warehouse into a revolver. So all it's really doing is putting everything that's originated by that group now into the warehouse group.

Michael Rose -- Raymond James -- Managing Director

OK, that's helpful. And then maybe as a follow-up, just as we think about loan growth, obviously, I know there are some moving parts with credit this quarter. You guys put up $86 million, that's about 5% annualized ex the warehouse, you had previously talked about 11%. How realistic is that goal, given your pipelines and the slower start of this year?

Mays Davenport -- Chief Financial Officer

Yeah. I still think we're going to stick with that, Michael, and see how this plays out. We could have grown more this quarter. When I look at it, obviously, we had the energy stuff throughout.

So that's the $43 million, $44 million there. Also in the energy book, in Q1, we actually originated $97 million of new energy credits in the quarter, which was a pretty good origination quarter for our energy group and this is all kind of a two plus three, three years to hedging on all of these kinds of new underwriting deals but also in the quarter at our election, we chose to exit $97 million worth of syndicated credits in oil and gas. We did that in two ways. We sold some off in the secondary market at par.

And others, as they came up for renewal, we elected not to renew our piece. That's what drove that, what used to be roughly 50-50 mix between syndicated loans and self-originated loans in oil and gas that are now being 80-20. Just strategically, as we've looked at where we've had problems in the oil and gas portfolio or in the bank has been on SNCs, particularly larger SNCs, and so it was a strategic shift on our part. We took the opportunity to grow a little slower and let roughly, outside of the problem loan resolutions, another $55 million or so of energy deals come off the books.

So we probably could have, not probably, we could have put up a bigger loan growth number, we just chose to execute our strategy. So in terms of just raw originations for the quarter, it was a really good origination quarter, probably one of our best ever. There were some outsized payoffs in CRE and a little bit in C&I but the long and short of it is, I think we're going to stick with the 11% based largely, Michael, on the rate origination.

Michael Rose -- Raymond James -- Managing Director

OK. That's helpful. And maybe one last one from me is, last quarter you guys had talked about a NIM in the mid-370 range. Clearly, above that this quarter, obviously, the rate hike helped.

What's your expectation as we move through the year and based on the expectation, what are your expectations for deposit betas and also future rate hikes?

Mays Davenport -- Chief Financial Officer

We saw a 7-basis-point increase in NIM. Three basis points of that was related actually to the shrinkage of the warehouse business at the lower NIM business. So when that shrinks, the overall NIM of the bank goes up, so that 3 basis points was related to that. I don't expect that to continue because we'll see an increase in the warehouse up to seasonal high in second quarter and third quarter.

So some of that may go away but then the remaining 4 basis points were related to just the remix of deposits as I talked about. We've continued to steer away from high-beta correspondent deposits, which are the most expensive source of deposits in the bank. So anything we can do to move away from that helps. So we moved, as I mentioned, into some time deposits, which will help us from an asset-sensitivity perspective and then also we've moved into non-interest-bearing deposits.

So all of those have a significant impact on NIM. So again, some of that will give back the second quarter of the warehouse but I see us continuing to be above 380 unless there is some significant change in our deposit mix, which I don't foresee. And then on deposit betas, as we continue to reposition a little bit longer term with some CDs, I think that those betas will come down a little bit. If we continue to grow loans as we say, that's the biggest challenge is that incremental dollar we have to go get to fund those new loans is the ones that are generally going to be the most expensive.

Now having said that, as we get out into the third quarter, we're going to have our new Treasury-management platform come on and we will have a big push for non-interest bearing deposits. So that should help us well going into the third and fourth quarter.

Operator

Thank you. And the next question comes from Matt Olney with Stephens.

Kevin Hanigan -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Yeah. Two things. The warehouse deals, and there was $144 million of those that moved over at quarter-end or moved over in the quarter, have a slightly lower coupon on them.

So it muted what would have been a slightly higher number, Matt, in C&A but I think since they're in there, it's a good proxy going forward.

Matt Olney -- Stephens -- Analyst

So I need you to clarify, Kevin, no impact on the C&I loan yield from some of the non-accrual reversals that you mentioned earlier?

Kevin Hanigan -- President and Chief Executive Officer

It helped a little bit because energy is in C&I, couple of basis points, but again I think we're going to get the rest of these cleared out here sooner rather than later. They're all up for sale. It's just we're going through the process on these last couple of ones that are of any size and whether they get through the process by the end of this quarter or bleeds into Q3, we will see as it all plays out.

Matt Olney -- Stephens -- Analyst

OK. That's helpful. And then going back to the deposit-cost commentary, you talked about making some good progress on growing the non-interest bearing DDAs. It sounds like there's more progress to come there but the deposit costs were up 9 basis points.

Kevin Hanigan -- President and Chief Executive Officer

No. I think the rates go up, we'll still be fighting for the next marginal dollar probably in our money market accounts, it's got a higher beta and it gets more expensive. All I think we're really saying is our focus, and really all we've done so far to drive this increase in non-interest-bearing deposits, was change incentives. We made that change coming out of the planning conference last August.

So we implemented in September and when we look at the fourth quarter last year, we grew non-interest bearing, it was $107 million or $108 million and so another 40-something. So roughly $155 million in the last two quarters is pretty darn good, especially in light of the fact when we did $108 million of non-interest-bearing deposit growth in Q4, we said a lot of that wasn't real, a lot of that was more like $65 million and we expected some of that to go away, it was a business or two being sold and they had the money still parked here at year-end that went out. So we've had two really solid quarters in a row and I think what Mays was saying is that's without the new Treasury-management platform. It's our sincere hope that we can accelerate that once we get this installed.

We've been working on it now for six or seven months and it should be ready to be beta tested in the early part of the summer. So we're expecting, having spent the money on this, we're expecting our guys to be able to broaden the sale.

Operator

And the next question comes from Brad Milsaps with Sandler O'Neill.

Brad Milsaps -- Sandler O'Neill -- Managing Director

Kevin, you addressed almost everything but wanted to follow up on the warehouse. I guess Fannie and DA are forecasting kind of a 20% increase in originations in the second quarter. OK. And in terms of volume?
