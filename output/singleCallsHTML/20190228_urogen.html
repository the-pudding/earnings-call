Thank you, operator. Good morning, everyone, and welcome to UroGen Pharma's fourth quarter and full-year 2018 financial results and business update conference call.

I will now turn the call over to Liz.

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

Thank you, Kate. Good morning, everyone, and thank you for joining our conference call.

As many of you may know, I joined the UroGen team in the beginning of January. So, this is my first call as CEO of the company. I am absolutely delighted to have joined UroGen at such a defining time in the Company's evolution. Since, I have not had a chance to personally introduce myself to everyone dialed into the call, I'd like to take this opportunity to briefly review my background and experience before diving into UroGen's recent progress and upcoming milestones for 2019.

I've been in this industry for over 30 years, and prior to joining UroGen, I most recently served as CEO of Novartis Oncology. Prior to Novartis, I was the Global President of Oncology at Pfizer, where I had multiple leadership positions, including President of Global Innovative Pharma for Europe, President of the Specialty Care Business Unit for North America, and President of US Oncology.

I started my healthcare career at J&J leading multiple businesses and built the Oncology Business Unit as Vice President and General Manager at Cephalon. The breadth and depth of my experience has prepared me for this role as President and CEO of UroGen, and I have quickly integrated into the organization. I'm extremely impressed with the team here and the tremendous progress that has been achieved to advance the potential of our RTGel technology, as we work toward building our Company as a global leader in uro-oncology.

Shortly after I joined UroGen, we were pleased to announce positive results of our lead investigational candidate, UGN-101 from our pivotal Phase 3 OLYMPUS trial for the non-surgical treatment of patients with low-grade upper tract urothelial cancer or LG UTUC. We were very encouraged by the durable complete responses observed and believe that the data continue to support the potential of UGN-101 to change the treatment paradigm for patients with current options consist of repetitive endoscopic surgical intervention or complete loss of the kidney.

We have received Fast Track, Orphan Drug and Breakthrough Therapy Designation from the FDA for UGN-101. We initiated a rolling submission of our new drug application or NDA in December 2018 and are intensely focused on completing the submission in the second half of 2019 with the potential approval and launch in the first half of 2020. If approved, UGN-101 would be the first drug approved for the non-surgical treatment of low-grade UTUC.

As we work toward a potential approval in the first half of 2020, commercial planning activities are advancing on schedule to ensure that our infrastructure is equipped to support our first commercial launch. We've hired a team of accomplished senior level executive with experience in commercial, marketing sales, market access and medical affairs, to oversee the efforts for UGN-101. This team has a track record of success with significant experience in urology.

In the near-term, we are focused on planning and analysis. Our commercial strategy will incorporate a multifaceted approach to support uro-oncology practices across the country. Finding urologists who treat UTUC is critical, and our research indicates that approximately 33% of urology accounts actually treat 90% of the patient population. As a result, we will implement a targeted account-based approach with a small nimble and highly experienced salesforce of approximately 50 individuals to support the unique needs of UGN-101.

We are also very focused on ensuring that we will support the physician office and make UGN-101 a seamless addition to their practice, following potential approval. Education and scientific exchange are critical components, and our medical affairs team will play a key role to make certain that healthcare professionals are appropriately educated to increase their comfort level with this potentially paradigm shifting therapy and ensure early market adoption upon potential approval.

To that end, our team and medical science liaisons or MSLs have been hired and will be prepared to execute on education, scientific exchange and support of our clinical team. Urothelial cancer is the ninth most common cancer today, and it is the most costly cancer on a per patient basis. We are conducting a comprehensive analysis to define the cost burden to the system for low-grade UTUC. Remember that the patient population for this disease is elderly. So if you think about comorbidities of hypertension and diabetes in these patients, you can imagine that the removal of the kidney, or repetitive endoscopic surgeries is very debilitating, giving us a very compelling case for UGN-101.

As we prepare for launch, market access as well as distribution and logistics are critically important. Our proactive strategy will help make sure that patients who need UGN-101 will be able to receive it and that physicians will be able to acquire UGN-101 when needed. We are confident in our ability to execute on our current work streams and build a highly efficient commercial organization. Our team will be fully prepared as we transition into a commercial company.

Subject to UGN-101's approval, it is important to note that as we progress our commercial planning, we are also setting the groundwork for UGN-102. Our investigational program for low-grade non-muscle invasive bladder cancer, which is a much larger patient population. We are already off to a strong start in 2019, with the completion of a follow-on public offering in January, raising approximately $162 million in net proceeds.

With our dynamic leadership team firmly in place, commercial preparation is well under way and with solid financial position, we are prepared for what is poised to be a defining year for UroGen. We are closing in on potential approval of our first therapy and continue to advance our pipeline programs, as well as to assess partnerships to further enhance the potential reach of our RTGel platform in uro-oncology and beyond. As we approach completion of our rolling NDA submission later this year, we look forward to updating you on the progress and planning activities.

I would now like to turn the call over to Mark to discuss in more detail our progress in clinical development programs. Mark?

Mark P. Schoenberg -- Chief Medical Officer

Thank you, Liz. As Liz just mentioned, we recently reported positive results from our pivotal Phase 3 OLYMPUS clinical trial of UGN-101 for the non-surgical treatment of low-grade UTUC. We observed a 57% complete response rate or CR, which was very consistent with the data that were previously presented in May 2018. Importantly, all evaluated patients in CR remain disease free at six months.

At the time of the announcement, approximately 50% of the patients that were in CR had reached the six-month time point, and we will continue to follow and evaluate these patients. The safety profile of UGN-101 continues to be acceptable with most treatment-emergent adverse events characterized as mild to moderate and transient and in line with urethral procedures.

We are encouraged by the high response rate and durability observed to date, which provide further evidence that the non-surgical treatment of low-grade UTUC with UGN-101 may result in clinically meaningful recurrence free survival. The treatment of low-grade UTUC represents a technical challenge for urologists, as the current standard of care requires surgical intervention, which puts patients at risk for the well-known complications associated with repetitive surgical procedures and potential kidney removal. Approximately 80% of patients who present with low-grade UTUC will undergo kidney removal at some point in their treatment, either at initial presentation or once repetitive endoscopic surgery fails to control the disease.

As we have previously discussed, these patients are generally elderly and are subject to the short-term risks associated with kidney removal surgery such as, bleeding, infection, injury to adjacent organs and the risks of anesthesia, and the effects of chronic renal insufficiency. Renal preservation in this population would address a significant unmet medical need.

In the OLYMPUS trial alone, 45% of patients presented with endoscopically unresectable tumors. In real practice, these patients would have been immediate candidates for kidney removal. This is another example of how UGN-101 has the potential to transform the front-line management of patients with low-grade UTUC.

As a urologist, I believe that UGN-101 has the potential to make a substantial impact on contemporary clinical practice. In addition to providing physicians with an opportunity to avoid the risks associated with repetitive endoscopic surgery, as well as those associated with kidney removal, UGN-101 fits nicely into current urology practice. It requires the use of equipment, procedures and protocols that are familiar to practicing urologic surgeons and aligns well with standard ambulatory practice.

Beyond UGN-101, we are equally excited about the potential for our UGN-102 program. We are currently enrolling patients in our OPTIMA II Phase 2b single-arm open label multicenter trial designed to assess the efficacy and safety of UGN-102 for the intravesical instillation, as a potential first-line chemoablation agent in the treatment of patients with recurrent low-grade non-muscle invasive bladder cancer.

Let me first describe this patient population. We have chosen to study intermediate risk patients. This is a patient population, whereby the current standard of care Transurethral Resection of Bladder Tumor or TURBT procedure is used repeatedly to address chronic recurrence of disease. These patients experience what can be viewed as a form of surgical failure, and many undergo multiple surgical procedures in real life, to manage bladder cancer recurrences. We believe UGN-102 can have an immediate impact on this population and provide a non-surgical alternative for the treatment of chronic relapse.

UGN-102 represents a substantial opportunity for advancing our RTGel technology platform in a larger patient population. Today, there are approximately 700,000 people living with bladder cancer in the United States, and approximately 80,000 who were classified as intermediate risk. Similar to low-grade UTUC, there are no currently approved drugs by the FDA as first-line treatment for low-grade non-muscle invasive bladder cancer.

We are pleased with the enrollment and anticipate a data update in 2019. While six month durability is important for low-grade UTUC, we believe that recurrence free survival at 12 months for low-grade bladder cancer is clinically meaningful. Thus, these data would consist of CR rates with durability information to be reported at a later time. We remain on track to complete enrollment this year.

We are also developing UGN-201, an earlier stage program that leverages our platform in immuno-oncology. We are currently evaluating various pathways to advance the program into clinical development for high grade disease. We plan to provide an update on the development path for UGN-201 later this year.

Finally, our last clinical program is a collaboration with Allergan. Allergan is conducting an ongoing Phase 2 trial of BotuGel, a combination of our RTGel and BOTOX for the treatment of overactive bladder or OAB. Current treatment with BOTOX for OAB requires approximately 40 to 60 injections directly into the bladder. BotuGel has the potential to change this treatment paradigm with a topical therapy versus multiple internal injections in the bladder. If successful, we believe this study will demonstrate the potential of our RTGel technology platform beyond the field of Uro-Oncology.

And with that, I would like to turn the call over to Peter, who will discuss the financials.

Peter P. Pfreundschuh -- Chief Financial Officer

Thank you, Mark, and good morning to all of you on today's call. UroGen is entering 2019 well capitalized and ready to support our clinical development programs and commercial planning efforts in preparation for a potential US approval of UGN-101 in 2020. We ended 2018 with $101.3 million in cash, cash equivalents, and short-term investments, and in January, we successfully completed the public offering of ordinary shares, resulting in net proceeds of approximately $162 million.

For the fourth quarter and full year ended December the 31, 2018, we reported net losses of $23.7 million or $1.46 per share and $75.7 million or $4.80 per share, as compared to $10.1 million or $0.74 per share and $20 million or $2.14 per share for the same periods in 2017.

Research and development expense for the fourth quarter and full year ended December 31, 2018 were $11.5 million and $36.9 million as compared to $6.8 million and $18.7 million for the same period in 2017. Research and development expenses include $3 million and $12 million of non-cash stock-based compensation expense for the fourth quarter and full-year ended December 31, 2018, as compared to $1.4 million and $3.9 million for the same period in 2017.

The increase in research and development expenses from 2017 to 2018 reflect an increase in direct costs associated with the UGN-101 Phase 3 OLYMPUS trial, cost to initiate the UGN-102 Phase 2b OPTIMA II trial, an increase in personnel costs to support our ongoing clinical and regulatory efforts and an increase in share-based compensation expense.

General and administrative expenses for the fourth quarter and full-year ended December 31, 2018 were $12.6 million and $39.6 million as compared to $3.4 million and a $8.8 million for the same periods in 2017. General and administrative expenses include $5.9 million and $18.6 million of non-cash stock-based compensation expense for the fourth quarter and full-year ended December 31, 2018, as compared to $0.9 million and $2.4 million for the same periods in 2017.

The increase in general and administrative expenses from 2017 to 2018 was attributable to an increase in personnel and related costs to support our growing business and increase in commercial infrastructure and services and increasing consultant and other outside fees, and an increase in share-based compensation expense.

As of December 31, 2018, we had approximately 16.2 million ordinary shares outstanding. Based on our anticipated activities, we project our current cash balance to carry us for the next 24 months to 36 months.

Post our January 2019 financing, we refreshed our best business plans to achieve the goals that we have set out for the Company in 2019. Based upon this, the Company anticipates a net loss in the range of $100 million to $150 million for 2019, which includes non-cash stock-based compensation expense in the range of $24 million to $27 million subject to market conditions.

The change in net loss for 2019 versus 2018 is largely attributable to the build-out of commercial infrastructure in anticipation of the potential launch of UGN-101. The company has 20.4 million ordinary shares outstanding, post the closing of our most recent financing in January.

With that operator, I would like to now turn the call over for questions.

Thank you. We will now begin the question-and-answer session. (Operator Instructions) And our first question comes from Ram Selvaraju from H. C. Wainwright. Please, go ahead.

Ram Selvaraju -- H. C. Wainwright & Co. -- Analyst

Hi. Thanks very much for taking my questions. I just wanted to know if it would be possible for you to provide us with some additional detail regarding the filing process for UGN-101?

Secondly, I wanted to see if you could comment further on how you expect the commercialization strategy for UGN-101 to differ from UGN-102? And if you could clarify for us whether looking forward to the potential future approval of UGN-102, you would be looking to deploy exactly the same sales -- infrastructure sales personnel for the support of the rollout of both products or if you anticipate there being any notable differences?

And finally, with respect to BotuGel, do you have any feedback directly from Allergan regarding what they intend to do if the next set of clinical data is positive, as one might expect it to be? And furthermore, to what extent they anticipate BotuGel being a component of their long-term franchise management strategy for BOTOX overall? Thank you.

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

Okay, great. Thanks. It's Liz Barrett talking -- speaking, just so you understand, I'm going to answer the last one first, because it's easy and we cannot comment on what their expectations are, what their plans are. So I think that's a question for Allergan.

To talk a little bit more about the filing process, it's in a rolling submission, you -- basically as the different components are ready, you're able to submit those to the FDA. We have already submitted the preclinical module. We are currently working on the CMC module, and that module will go in next, and then the final module that will go in will be the clinical module.

Our timing for this final submission is really driven by the fact that we have to wait for the majority of the patients to reach the six-month durability milestone, because that was the requirement by the FDA that we needed to have the majority of the patients in -- at least 75% of the patients at six months. The six-month -- and Mark can obviously add any commentary here, was really driven by the fact that the patient population -- average time of recurrence is six months. And so, that's why the FDA felt like six-month data was really critical importance.

So, the only really rate-limiting factor for finalizing the filing, the final submission is just simply as I said, waiting for those patients to reach that milestone. From UGN-101 launch, I would say we're very excited about all of the work that's been done. I think 2019 is going to be a very busy year for us, as we spin the year, building awareness and educating the physician population through appropriate medical education.

When you think about the strategy of UGN-101 and UGN-102, I would say similar in the sense of -- obviously, it's the same physician group. The difference would be that -- with UGN-102 it would be a broader physician population that we would be reaching because there obviously are more patients and more physicians treating these patients. So we do believe at this point in time, we would have to expand the current commercial organization to reach more physicians at that point in time. So that's -- I think that would be a bit how they differ, the actual products are different, the therapies are different and instillation method is different. And so again, similarities -- a lot of similarities, but obviously, a lot of differences as well.

So hopefully that answered all your questions. I think I didn't (ph) missed anything. If I did, please let me know.

Ram Selvaraju -- H. C. Wainwright & Co. -- Analyst

No, just a point of clarification regarding the filing. I don't know if you plan to provide specific notifications of the completions of the submissions of different sections of the NDA as they go in, or if you're just going to notify us when the NDA submission is fully complete?

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

We would not. There's really no reason, to be honest with you to do so. It doesn't mean that if you're interested in learning, we're happy to let you know individually, but there would be no to public disclosure of that.

Ram Selvaraju -- H. C. Wainwright & Co. -- Analyst

Okay. Thank you very much.

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

Sure. Thank you.

Operator

Our next question comes from Boris Peaker from Cowen. Please go ahead.

Boris Peaker -- Cowen & Co. -- Analyst

Great. Good morning. My first question is on UGN-101. Just curious, when will we see 12 months durability data, or is that something that you anticipate to report altogether?

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

Yes. We won't be sharing any additional information until the filing, I mean until the trial is complete. We will show at some point, obviously 12 months durability data, but it's not an endpoint for us. And so, before we would have all of that data, it would really be mid to late 2020. So -- but yes, we will absolutely -- after we finalize the FDA submission and the data package is final, we will be publishing and hopefully presenting the full dataset that we have, including data on the 12 months and the number of patients we have 12 months data on hopefully by the end of the year.

Boris Peaker -- Cowen & Co. -- Analyst

Great. And my second question on UTUC, and this is just kind of a patient management question that, if a patient comes in with UTUC and their kidney function is fine, both of the kidneys, why not just remove one of the kidneys on the side of the tumor and eliminate any risk of further progression since the other kidney could takeover. I don't know why wouldn't physician just choose that option?

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

Yes. I'm going to let Mark answer that question as a practicing urologist. He has a lot of experience in this area. Mark?

Mark P. Schoenberg -- Chief Medical Officer

Thanks, Liz. Boris, that's a great question. From a clinical perspective, a couple of facts to keep in mind. The risk of the patient developing a similar malignancy in the remaining kidney is somewhere around 2% to 5%. So that kidney remains at risk actually even when the original tumor has been removed with the primary side of development. So, there is ongoing disease risk that would potentially compromise the remaining kidney.

The other thing to keep in mind is really related to the age of the patient population affected by this disease. And I know that you're aware, as our audience on the call that the average age is 74 when this disease is diagnosed. When you take the kidney -- when you take a kidney out of a 74-year-old who's kidney function appears to be normal and perfect for that age, there still is a downstream risk approximately 30% of that person will develop particularly significant renal insufficiency. So there is a benefit to preserving kidney mass through a renal preserving therapeutic strategy that we're proposing with UGN-101. And because of this disease tends to be a chronic relapsing illness, but not one that metastasizes the benefit to the patient of preserving the kidney actually is very significant and the risk of developing a widely metastatic disease is actually very small. So, the overall clinical benefit is to keep the kidney in the person.

Boris Peaker -- Cowen & Co. -- Analyst

Great. Thank you for taking my question.

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

Thank you. Next question.

Operator

Our next question comes from Derek Archila from Stifel. Please go ahead.

Hi. Bill on for Derek. Thanks for taking our question. With regards to VesiGel, where you are at in discussions with the FDA? Could the open-label Phase 2 service a registrational study or do you think you'll need to run a Phase 3 versus TURBT or -- and if you do, does it have to be superior or just non-inferior? Thanks.

Liz Barrett -- President & Chief Executive Officer

No, it's a great question. I would say that we anticipate that we will have to conduct another study given the feedback that we've had so far from the FDA in the current study. My only caveat to that, is that always depends on what the data looks like, and as Mark explained and described the patient population, this is an intermediate-risk patient population. So they are at a higher risk for recurrence than your overall patient population. So depending on the results of this study and we will absolutely have a discussion with the FDA to see what study design that might look like. So again, we anticipate, we will have to conduct another study, what that study looks like, will be highly dependent on the data that we see coming out of this current study.

Bill -- Stifel -- Analyst

Great. Thank you.
