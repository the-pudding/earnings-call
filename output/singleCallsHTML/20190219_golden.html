Prepared Remarks:

Operator

Ladies and gentlemen, thank you for standing by, and welcome to the Q4 2018 Golden Ocean Group Limited Earnings Conference Call. At this time, all participants are in a listen-only mode. (Operator Instructions) I must advise you that today's conference is being recorded on Tuesday, February 19th, 2019.

And I should now hand over to your first host for today, Birgitte Vartdal. Please go ahead.

Birgitte Ringstad Vartdal -- Chief Executive Officer

Good learning, and good afternoon. I'm sorry for the slight delay in start time. Welcome to the fourth quarter 2018 earnings call for Golden Ocean Group Limited. My name is Birgitte Vartdal, I'm the CEO of Golden Ocean Management, and together with me I have Per Heiberg, CFO of Golden Ocean Management.

We are pleased with our results for the fourth quarter and full year of 2018, despite a drop in the Capesize rates in the end of the quarter. As most of you know, the market has weakened dramatically since the start of the year, and particularly, following the tragic dam disaster in Brazil. We will comment on the market after Per has taken you through the Company update. Please go ahead Per.

Per Heiberg -- Chief Financial Officer

Okay. Thank you, Birgitte. Golden Ocean reported a net income will turn to $23.6 million and earnings per share of $0.16 for the fourth quarter of 2018 compared to $35.3 million and $0.24 in the third quarter. Adjusted EBITDA ended at $70.4 million, down from 78.8 million in the previous quarter.

The Company announced a share buyback program in December 2018, to purchase up to an aggregate of 6 million shares at a maximum price per share of $9. So far, the Company has bought 570,000 shares at a total cost of $3.4 million under the program. We have also secured financing for up to 11 scrubber installations through one of our existing loan facilities. The facility has been extended by three years until June 2023, and increased with an extra tranche of up to $33 million for the scrubber installations.

The Company's only long-term corporate debt, the $200 million convertible bond, was repaid with cash at maturity in late January this year. The Company announced a dividend of $0.05 per share for the fourth quarter of 2018. For the full year, total announced dividends amounts to $0.40 per share.

Moving on to the P&L, the time charter equivalent, or TCE, revenues decreased by $2.4 million compared to the previous quarter. The decrease is mainly due to the rates on our Capesize vessel offset by higher activity on short-term trading with third-party vessels and good performance from the smaller sized vessels. The increased trading activity is also reflected in the $4 million higher charter hire expenses compared to the previous quarter.

Ship operating expenses increased by $1.8 million compared to last quarter. The increase is primarily a result of slightly higher operating cost across the fleet and higher cost for docking in the quarter compared to the previous one. (inaudible) was stable at $23.3 million, since we did not have any changes to the fleet. (Technical Difficulty)

Adjusted EBITDA came in at $70.4 million for the quarter, and the Company achieved a TCE per day of $17,525, a slight decrease from the previous quarter. The TCE was significantly above the Company's long-term cash break even levels including full debt service resulting in a strong operational cash flow in the quarter.

Moving onto the cash flow statement or showing the cash flow, we ended the quarter with $368.4 million and generated a positive cash flow from operations of $60.7 million. $30.2 million of debt was paid during the quarter and the amount consist of $11.6 million paid under the cash sweep mechanism of the Company's non-recourse debts, $2 million repurchase of the Company's convertible bond and the rest is ordinary amortization of long-term debt. The Company paid $21.6 million, or $0.15 per share in dividend in fourth quarter. Other cash outflow relate mainly to the purchase of own shares and installments and the Company's scrubber purchases.

In the balance sheets, we see that cash, including short and long-term restricted cash, amounted to $372.6 million at the end of the quarter. Other current assets went down by $26.7 million, partly in conjunction with a $18.4 million decrease in other current liabilities, with the difference relating to the decrease in value of other financial assets. The book value of the Company's assets decreased with ordinary depreciation of $23 million in the quarter.

The current portion of the Company's long-term debt increased by $227 million over the quarter and this increase mainly relate to one loan facility that matures late December 2019, and some tranches of other facilities that mature in the fourth quarter of 2019. This comes in addition to the maturity of the convertible bond that was repaid at the -- that have been repaid already. We expect to be able to refinance the facilities that mature prior to maturity as attractive terms for the Company. At the end of the quarter, the Company's book equity was around 52%.

On the graph showing the OpEx, we see that the yearly average daily OpEx for the two vessel classes remain in the fleet. These costs include fully burdened cost of drydock and management fee and show that OpEx is stable at around $5,200 per day on average for all vessels, regardless of size. Cost for drydock expenses is expensed as incurred and add to the running OpEx in our P&L. During third quarter, the Company drydocked only one Panamax vessel. And for the entire year, we drydocked six vessels in total. In 2019, 19 vessels, consisting of 16 Capes and 3 Panamaxes are expected to be drydocked.

The graph to the right of the slide shows an overview of the vessels with and without ballast water treatment systems installed. As you can see, more than 50% of the Company's vessels already have these systems installed, and the cost of the remaining installments is spread from this year until 2023, with a total estimated cost of around $33 million.

The Company did not enter into any further commitments with respect to scrubber program in the fourth quarter. The program currently consists of 20 firm and five optional scrubber installations projects. The scrubber installation schedule is shown on the graph to the right and all committed scrubber installations will coincide with scheduled vessels drydocks in 2019 and into first half of 2020.

Looking at the coverage and the fleet, the Company's core fleet was unchanged over the quarter and consists of 77 sailing vessels, of which, 46 are Capes, 16 are Panamaxes/Kamsarmaxes, 12 Ice-class Panamaxes, and three Ultramaxes. Since the last earning report, the Company has entered into one long term contract for one of the Panamax vessels. Current fixed rate cover for our Capesize fleet in 2019 is equivalent of three vessels at a gross rate of $20,965. This has been achieved by converting, one of the index-linked vessels to fixed rate and selling some FFA contracts.

In addition, we have entered into five floor and ceiling contracts for 2019 and two for 2020, securing the downside approximately $15,000 per day by giving away the upside about $29,500. The settlement on these options are based on monthly averages and today's weak market demonstrate the effectiveness of this structure as we secure the floor on these contracts of approximately $15,000 in an $8,000 market.

On the Panamax fleet, 14 vessels have started out on fixed term contracts for the winter, securing the maturity of first half of 2019 at an average rate of $16,900. Seven of these contracts last for a longer period and expire during 2020 and 2021, at an average rate of $19,400.

That ends the presentation and I hand over the word to Birgitte, who will take you through the market development section.

Birgitte Ringstad Vartdal -- Chief Executive Officer

Thank you, Per. Since the late summer of '18, number of events have put the dry bulk markets under pressure. The trade war between US and China, which began as a war of word opened the seasonal dynamics of the grain trade. More importantly, it negatively impacted the sentiments and has created a significant uncertainty at the time when the markets have begun to show good strength. Recent headlines offer (Technical Difficulty) and the market continues to wait for a resolution.

Towards the end of January '19, a devastating tragedy occurred, when the Vale tailings dam in Brumadinho, Brazil failed, causing significant loss of life. Following this event, Vale announced that it would decommission all of its upstream tailing dams. Together with Brumadinho, this had the effect of decreasing iron ore outputs by 70 million ton. As iron ore is primary transported on (inaudible) the immediate impact was severe.

This also occurred around the start of Chinese New Year celebrations. And during this period, which lasts for up to three weeks, economic activity is practically shut down in China. Suffice to say that the drybulk markets have been put under pressure by a confluence of events at a time when underlying sentiments for the short term was not very strong.

As mentioned in the previous call, the fourth quarter was weaker than expected, following a strong third quarter. This also reflects in drop of the utilization, which dropped to approximately 86% in the quarter, mainly due to a drop in demand as fleet capacity was flat quarter-over-quarter.

Although demand decreased in the fourth quarter of '18, it was still up year-over-year and followed the seasonal trends seen over the last few years. The drop in coal transportation of the main reason for decrease in transport demand and a consequence of implementations of import restrictions into China. Transport of non-agribulks (ph) were also a bit down, while other commodities were up.

Despite the drop in demand of iron ore, steel production in China continued its good growth path in the fourth quarter of '18. This was in contrast to the unchanged year-over-year production levels in the rest of the world. As mentioned on many calls before, stockpiles of steel continue to be at modest levels in China, which is an indication that steel is being -- that is being produced is also being consumed.

Following the drop in steel margins going into the fourth quarter, the margins stabilized toward the end of the year and into '19. After the dam accident in Brazil, iron ore prices started to rise sharply again on the back of lack of iron ore available. This has had a natural effect of compressing steel margins in the short term, although we anticipate iron ore prices will stabilize as lost production is being replaced.

Looking at the fourth quarter, iron ore export volumes from Australia and Brazil decreased. There was of course an high expectation going into 2019 that additional iron ore volumes to the market will common on stream from Brazil based on previous guidance. Vale was estimated to increase the production by 25 million ton to 30 million ton from 2018 levels. The tragic accident, put a short-term lid on that optimism .

Beyond the production cuts that have already been announced by Vale, there is uncertainty about what the Brazilian Government will require for Vale to improve safety in response to the accident, and what other measures Vale will take on their own. This has created uncertainty around the export volumes expected for '19, and it looks like no one has a totally clear picture and a lot of numbers are circulating around.

Starting with the 2018 production levels and the estimated improved guidance that was in place earlier for 2019, the deduction of up to 70 million tons from Brumadinho and other sources lead to a 40 million ton reduction compared to '18. There are assumptions that Vale will be able to replace part of that from their Northern System. And as a potential indication, you can see 20 million tons being recouped in other areas.

In addition, Anglo's Rio Minas were out of the market during most of 2018. It is now back on stream and should add another 15 million tons compared to the year. Adding that together, you should be more or less underlining again that these are very uncertain estimates, but you should be more or less back on the 2018 volumes in total if this is the final scenario. Any shortfall will then have to be compensated from other areas.

Australia should have a capacity of 10 million tons to 20 million tons, but obviously, it's a fraction of the distance to China compared to Brazil. And the replacement from Australia will have a negative ton mileage. Bearing in mind the higher iron ore prices, as mentioned earlier, we expect to see also other suppliers being able to potentially add some volumes to the market.

While this clearly have a significant short-term effect on the month, it's too early to conclude on the potential impact for the right outlook for the whole of 2019.

Moving on to coal, while total coal volumes transported have been healthy, there was a sharp drop as illustrated on the graph to the left, in the fourth quarter following Chinese import restrictions that (Technical Difficulty) imports to 2017 levels, possibly a measure to support domestic production and prices and in view of higher coal inventories in China. January volumes, however, surged back to 33.5 million ton, up 23.3 million tons from December and also up year-over-year. Part of this increase is a result of lifting import restrictions at the start of the year and what's been seen in relation to lower volumes at the end of last year and cargo being on vessels waiting to discharge.

Volumes into India continued to grow in 2018 as the energy requirements expanded and as you can see from the graph, India also has lower coal inventories.

Despite what we read about a more cautious economic activity in China, electricity production continues to grow and had a strong 7% growth year-over-year in the fourth quarter. Thermal coal was 73% of the electricity production and therefore, continues to be the most important part of China's energy mix. As coal imports slowed in the fourth quarter, we saw domestic production increase and compensated for the shortfall in imported volumes in that period.

Moving on to grains, unfortunately, the recent shutdown of the US Federal Government has led to limited export availability for the fourth quarter. But as we know, the US-China trade war had significant effect on the quarter, which is normally high season for US grain exports. Volumes were down 2.4% year-over-year on agri in general, leading to less activity in the Panamax sector.

At the start of the first quarter this year, there have been some reports of Chinese buyers of US soybeans to support the discussions on the trade war, but in total, these volumes are far less than volumes last year. Looking ahead, we are waiting for East Coast/South America harvest season to start toward the end of the quarter, which should be supportive to Panamax rates.

Moving on to the supply side, fleet growth was somewhat lower in the fourth quarter of 2018 than in the earlier quarters. For 2018 as a whole, the fleet grew by 28 million deadweight ton, which is around 3.5% of the fleet. Scraping was almost non-existent in the year, with only -- with less than 5 million deadweight tons or 0.6% of the fleet being taken out of the market based on the optimistic outlook and improvement in rates having been seen.

Looking ahead, the order book represents around 11.5% of the fleet on the water, this is low in historic terms for 2019, but various sources on the order book has different estimates, the highest one is 5.9% gross deliveries as seen here. We expect to see lower deliveries, which I will come back to in the next slide, but we should also see off hire in relation to drydocking on scrubber installations, as well as an expectation of higher scrapping due to the current market environment combined with high docking costs due to regulations. As an example, we have so far this year seen seven Capes been scrapped or sold for scrap compared to 18 scraps -- 18 vessels for the full year of 2018.

These graphs, as we have had before, shows the distribution between orders not commenced, vessels under construction and vessels launched. As you can see from the number, 36% of the vessels marks of scheduled for delivery in 2019 has not even commenced construction. And obviously for vessels scheduled in the first half of '19, which have not commence construction, you will at least see a delay, if not the cancellation.

In the order book, there are several old orders. A number of vessels representing around 13 million deadweight tons were ordered in 2015 or earlier. And these -- maybe some of these vessels that are in the order book, that have not commenced and some of them have been in place at yards that have since gone bankrupt. Based on the current markets, you may also expect to see some owners trying to push deliveries out in time.

Prices in the S&P market have been slightly lower, but very few transactions have taken place to reflect the latest developments in the market. We would expect to see a drop in asset values, but at the moment, both the buyers and sellers are standing off and not a lot is transacted or being circulated for sale.

Going into 2020, we maintain our view that modern assets are the most attractive to buyers and we expect to continue to see a widening of the price spread between modern and older tonnage.

So, looking back at last year, rates were up compared to 2017. However, toward the end of the year, as discussed, the fourth quarter disappointed compared to expectation. The impact of the trade war and coal import restriction into China, capped the market in the fourth quarter and going into 2019, we observe good volumes. And Cape rates were actually stable around $15,000 per day during January. Panamax and smaller vessels dropped back due to a lack of grain out of the US and also restrictions on coal imports.

There was uncertainties looming around the trade war but also optimism in relation to future stimulus packages. The accident In Brazil quickly changed the sentiment and at this stage, we believe many negative factors listed here are priced into the spot market and to the forward curve.

Setting aside the market volatility and the current market weakness, we believe that our continued focus on low cash breakeven levels and the strong balance sheet provides us with a very strong footing. And our modern fleet improves our earnings potential, which will be particularly important as we approach 2020, and in an environment were higher fuel prices are virtually certain. Despite the short-term volatility and uncertainty, with all factors considered, we believe the upside potential outweighs the downside risk.

We will continue to focus on returning value to shareholders through dividends and potential share repurchases and for this quarter, the Board has declared a dividend of $0.05 per share, a reflection on the strong fourth quarter results, but also an acknowledgment of the current weaker market. And we will continue to aim in finding the right balance between returning value to shareholders and other uses of cash flow, including deleveraging and also always considering potential investments.

This ends the presentation for today and we are open to answer questions you may have. Thank you.
