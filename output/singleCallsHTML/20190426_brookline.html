Brookline Bancorp Inc (NASDAQ:BRKL)

Prepared Remarks:

Operator

Good day, and welcome to the Brookline Bancorp First Quarter 2019 Earnings Conference Call. All participants will be in listen-only mode. (Operator Instructions) After today's presentation, there will be an opportunity to ask questions. (Operator Instructions) Please note today's event is being recorded.

I would now like to turn the conference over to Lindsey Kitchens. Please go ahead.

Lindsey Dattoli Kitchens -- Senior Law Clerk

Thank you, Rocco. Good afternoon, everyone, and welcome to Brookline Bancorp Inc's first quarter 2019 earnings conference call. Yesterday, we issued our earnings release, which is available on the Investor Relations page of our website, brooklinebancorp.com, and has been filed with the SEC. This afternoon's call will be hosted by Brookline Bancorp's executive team, Paul A. Perrault and Carl M. Carlson.

Before we begin, please note, this call may contain forward-looking statements with respect to the financial conditions, results of operations and business of Brookline Bancorp. Actual results may differ from these forward-looking statements. Factors that may cause actual results to differ include those identified in our annual report on Form 10-K, our most recently filed 10-Q and our earnings press release. Brookline Bancorp cautions you against unduly relying upon any forward-looking statements and disclaims any intent to update publicly any forward-looking statements, whether in response to new information, future events or otherwise.

Any references made during this presentation to non-GAAP measures are only made to assist you in understanding Brookline Bancorp's results and performance trends and should not be relied on as a financial measures of actual results or future predictions. For a comparison and reconciliation to GAAP earnings, please see our earnings release.

And now I'm pleased to introduce Brookline Bancorp's President and CEO, Paul Perrault.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Thanks, Lindsey. Good afternoon all. I'm accompanied today by our Chief Financial Officer, Carl Carlson, who will walk you through our quarterly financial results following my comments. I'm pleased to report we had another strong quarter with quality organic growth in loans and deposits. For the quarter, loan balances grew $85 million and deposits grew by $167 million. Our margin was stable and we had solid quarter for fee income.

Yesterday, our Board of Directors approved a $0.005 increase in our quarterly dividend to $0.11 which represents a 10% increase over last year. We reported earnings of $22.5 million or $0.28 per share for the first quarter. Adjusting 2018 for some securities gains and merger charges, net income was up 12% year-over-year. We continue to execute our strategies, growing organically in the markets that we serve. We have a great group of employees dedicated to making Brookline Bancorp one of the region's leading commercial banking enterprises.

I will now turn you over to Carl who will review the Company's first quarter.

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

Thank you, Paul. As Paul mentioned, loans grew $84.7 million in the first quarter, 5.4% on an annualized basis with growth coming in all major loan categories. Loan originations and drawdowns in the quarter were $392 million with an average weighted coupon of 5.85%. The weighted average yield on the loan portfolio for the quarter was 509 basis points, an increase of 5 basis points from the fourth quarter. The overall yield on earning assets rose 7 basis points to 4.84%. Deposits grew $166.6 million during the quarter with growth coming primarily in certificates of deposit.

During the quarter the cost of interest-bearing deposits increased 17 basis points to 1.44% from the fourth quarter. The strong growth in deposits provided the opportunity to reduce our wholesale borrowings by $54.5 million. Our margin increased 6 basis points from Q4 to 3.64%, in part due to the reduced number of days in the quarter. Our earning assets grew and our margin improved in the quarter while our net interest income declined to $160,000 on a linked quarter basis due to 2 fewer days in Q1 versus Q4. Included in net interest income is the impact of purchase accounting and prepayment fees. Purchase accounting was $313,000 in the first quarter down $71,000 from the fourth quarter, and prepayment fees were $1.1 million consistent with the fourth quarter. Combined, the quarter-over-quarter changes had no incremental impact on the change in margins during the quarter.

Non-interest income was $6.6 million in the first quarter, up $169,000 from Q4. The increase was driven by the positive mark-to-market of $134,000 on the equity portfolio versus the negative market of $692,000 in the fourth quarter. Our provision for credit losses for the quarter was $1.4 million as charges for the quarter largely went against established specific reserves and favorable credit trends resulted in allowance for loan losses of $58 million, representing 91 basis points on loans.

During the quarter, non-accrual loans declined $1.3 million to $22.8 million or 36 basis points of total loans. Net charge-offs were $2.1 million or 13 basis points on an annualized basis. Excluding the impact of merger and acquisition expense from Q4, the Company's non-interest expense declined $885,000 from the fourth quarter to $38.9 million. The decline was driven by lower OREO-related expenses and lower incentive-related expenses. Yesterday, the Board approved a quarterly common dividend of $0.11 per share which will be paid on May 24, to stockholders of record on May 10, and represents an annualized yield of 2.96%.

That concludes our formal statements. We will now open it up for questions.

Questions and Answers:

Operator

Thank you. We will now begin the question-and-answer session. (Operator Instructions) Today's first question comes from Laurie Hunsicker of Compass Point. Please go ahead.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Yes, hi. Good afternoon. Just a couple of things, just quickly on the credit side. Obviously, your pristine. Just hoping you could give a couple of details to update us on the taxi book, which I know keeps winding down, just where is the balance? Where is the specific reserves?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

Sure. So, our total taxi book is right around $12 million. And just recall there's an $8.8 million relationship there that's we consider more of a commercial loan not necessarily a taxi, there's taxi that's collateral, there is other additional collateral around that. So when you exclude that, we look at the true taxi pieces is $3.2 million of taxi exposure. And we've got reserves of $1.3 million against that $3.2 million.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Okay. And then your charge-offs this quarter the $2.1 million, I assume that's all taxi?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

No, there's at -- $1.1 million of that was taxi.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Okay, got it. And then, how much of the taxi nonperformers at this point? How much of your $5.7 million, is most of that the taxi?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

I'd say most of that is the $3 million -- the $3.2 million.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Okay. And then just one more question here. The TRs that are accruing, we saw bit of a jump here this quarter. It went from $12.3 million last quarter to $28.5 million. Was there anything that was a stand out there? Or can you tell us a little bit about that directional increase?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

We basically have three credits that drove that number that we -- that are basically in workout at this time. And I'd say largely, in majority of that, we have no issue with as far as the ultimate collectibility of those and they're on accruing -- as I just said they're on accruing status.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

I'm sorry, what type of loans are they?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

What type of loans are they? There are some commercial loans -- there's a commercial loan in there and there's also real estate.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Okay. Okay. Great. And then, just over here on deposit costs, can you just talk a little bit about your money market and how we should be thinking about that going forward? And just overall how you're seeing, I mean, your cost of core deposit stayed lower than some in your market, just how we should be thinking about that going forward?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

I think we've seen a large degree of the movement in rates around the money market and the non-maturity deposits. I don't see that being -- there being as much pressure in that -- in those categories going forward. If anything, we actually start to see some folks come back off of some rates in those products, particularly when specials are being offered. I think the attention has really turned and particularly for our customers, have turned to trying to lock in some rates on the CD side.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Okay. All right, great. And then Paul just last question for you. Can you give us a little color around your current thinking on approaching M&A with your stock at these levels or what you've seen in the market? Just how you're thinking about it? If you can just refresh us on your thoughts, would be great. Thanks.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Well, as before, Laurie, I mean we don't feel like we're under the -- going to have to do anything as we're growing pretty well organically. So things are good. We have had exposure to virtually all of the recent transactions that have occurred in our neighborhood. And for a variety of reasons, we are not the purchaser of those. I think you can expect it will continue to be active in the search and pretty conservative in our execution.

Laurie Hunsicker -- Compass Point -- Analyst

Okay. Thank you.

Operator

And our next question today comes from Mark Fitzgibbon of Sandler O'Neill & Partners. Please go ahead.

Mark Fitzgibbon -- Sandler O'Neill and Partners -- Analyst

Hey, guys, good afternoon.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Hi, Mark.

Mark Fitzgibbon -- Sandler O'Neill and Partners -- Analyst

I notice that cash and short term investment balances have been on the rise for a while over the last few quarters, it's gone up pretty significantly, I think it's over $112 million. What's the plan for deploying that? How much cash do you think you need or should have in terms of short-term need?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

That balance will fluctuate from time to time, but it's something that we'll continue to manage that down. And we'd be able to reduce borrowings along the way.

Mark Fitzgibbon -- Sandler O'Neill and Partners -- Analyst

Okay. Secondly, the margin did a bit better than the guidance that you all had talked about last quarter. What are the big deltas and how are you thinking about the margin as we look ahead?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

I would say -- I don't think we anticipated the demand in CDs in our modeling per se. So that was a pleasant surprise and we've been able to take advantage of that, being able to raise funds in a term structure, the way we want in our asset -- for our asset liability purposes. And been able to replace our borrowings which will cost more money. And so that was probably the biggest factor in how the margin turned out.

Mark Fitzgibbon -- Sandler O'Neill and Partners -- Analyst

And then going forward?

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Well I'd like to be able to predict more of the same.

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

(Multiple Speakers) But the market has been good. And so the question is how much more of that's going to happen or did you see the big waves already? We'll see how that goes. And we have seen a backup in rates. So as far as the anticipation, rates may not be going up, they may be going down. As you know, the slight yield curve is going to be tougher, and so everybody's in the market. It's a lot more fun I would say these days. Well, retail folks may not think it's fun, but it's lot more fun these days where you're not just talking money market accounts, you're actually able to position yourself in the CD market and compete with deposits.

Mark Fitzgibbon -- Sandler O'Neill and Partners -- Analyst

Okay. And then with Easter now fully owned, is it likely that growth ramps up there? And how big might you let that business become over time?

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

No, I expect that there will be no change, it's the same personnel who have been there for many years who are continuing their good work. They see good growth every year. I expect that, that will continue. And they may inch up proportionally, I believe they're about 16% of our loan portfolio today. And if history repeats itself, that'll go up a little bit over the next year or two. And I have said in the past that I'm very, very comfortable to go to at least 20% of loans. And if we got that high, I'd revisit that and decide what the right kind of move is.

Mark Fitzgibbon -- Sandler O'Neill and Partners -- Analyst

Thank you.

Operator

(Operator Instructions) Today's next question comes from Matthew Breese of Piper Jaffray. Please go ahead.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Good afternoon.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Hi, Matt.

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

Hi, Matt.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Carl, I just want to make sure I understand the funding composition, at least the incremental funding. Should we anticipate similarly high levels of CD growth and therefore some pretty high level of overall deposit growth with the offset being lower borrowings from here, for at least the near term?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

Like -- I'll reiterate what Paul said. I'd love to say that but I'm not saying that.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Okay. Well, can you give us an idea of where you have seen price reductions? Let's call it in...

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Well, what it is. Matt, let me see if I can try to bring a little bit of clarity to it. The bad news is we have significant wholesale borrowings. The good news is we have lot of wholesale borrowings. So, we do have a rate environment that we've seen here during the late winter and spring, where depositors are interested at these rate levels to lock in for some term. Carl is able to work with our brand systems to put out there what are in the marketplace rather attractive rates, the terms that work for these guys in the algo methodology. And because it has displaced the wholesale borrowing, we have been able to attract kind of more than our fair share. Will that continue? I don't, know. I mean, it might but it might not and we may to go back to the normal size deposit growth. It's very hard to predict.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Okay. All right, understood. And then just thinking about the loan pipeline, loan growth was right in line with where you think this quarter. Any major changes in the pipeline that would make you more or less optimistic about forward growth?

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Probably a little bit more optimistic. It feels very strong. I've been to loan meetings here in April, and I'm very pleased with what I'm seeing.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Could you give us a little bit more color on where you're seeing in terms of loan categories, where the activity is?

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

It's across the commercial spectrum. Equipment financing continues to be very active. Real estate, obviously our legacy business continues to be very strong and we're beginning to see fruit from our expansion of the C&I world over the past year or so. You've heard us talk about that establishment of the two LPOs outside of Boston, are substantially staffed and they're beginning to produce.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Understood. Okay. And then just...

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

And those are -- those are C&I. I thought I didn't say that. I meant to say that last part.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Right. And then just lastly in regards to CECL, I'm not sure if you can provide any color on the initial true-up reserve and where that might shake out.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

That is painful. I don't mean financially, I mean the work is.

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

Yes, be careful.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Yes, the work is.

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

We're not in a position or would we want to be in a position to actually start talking about numbers yet. We are in very good shape as far as the data side, the modeling side, and we've been running some scenarios and more to come.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Just one follow-up there. You're working on the data side, just curious, how far back does your historical look back though when you're capturing those historical loss count -- those historical loss numbers?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

What's very nice is Brookline Bank has been keeping very good track of that information for decades. So, we have a lot of good historical data there. As you know, how many years ago now, the acquisition of Bank Rhode Island and First Ipswich came into the next. And so that data has been more recently accumulated. So we're in very good shape on that. The challenge -- the biggest challenge for Brookline Bancorp is the lack of actual losses. So how you calibrate for the market and for the industry and everything else is something that we will continue to be working through. Because if we actually used our own historical loss data, no one would believe what the reserves are. I'll leave it at that.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Uptown problem.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Well, I was looking at that, I mean, you have close to 10 years worth of historical charge-offs in your current reserve. How do you support bringing it higher under CECL if you have that much already there?

Carl M. Carlson -- Chief Financial Officer

I'm not going to applying on that right now, I think that's something we'll be working with our regulators and our accountants on.

Matthew Breese -- Piper Jaffray -- Analyst

Understood. Thanks for taking my questions.

Paul A. Perrault -- President and Chief Executive Officer

Sure.

Operator

And our next question comes from Collyn Gilbert of KBW. please go ahead.

Thanks. Good afternoon, guys.

So, I presume you're getting terms or durations as long enough on here to sort of offset the borrowings because I thought part of your borrowing strategy has always been -- yes, you get terms that allows you to fund sort of the longer duration asset portion of the balance sheet.
